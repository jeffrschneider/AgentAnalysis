Agentic Reengineering

A step-by-step guide to redesigning workflows with AI agents, producing detailed Agent Stories for implementation.

What You're Trying to Do

Where Agentic Replica documents how things work today, Agentic Reengineering imagines how they could work tomorrow. You're taking the raw material from your Replica and asking: if we had AI agents as partners, what would this role look like?

This isn't about replacing humans. It's about thoughtfully deciding which tasks benefit from automation, which benefit from AI assistance, and which should remain fully human. The output is a set of Agent Stories that serve as blueprints for implementation.

Key principle: Keep this separate from documenting the current state. You need to think freely about what's possible without being constrained by "but that's not how we do it today."

Before You Start

Agentic Reengineering builds on the work from Agentic Replica. Before starting, you should have:

  • A completed Agentic Replica documenting the current role, including all skills, triggers, inputs, processes, and outputs.
  • Cross-cutting patterns identified, especially escalation patterns and observed guardrails, which inform where human oversight is essential.
  • Pain points noted during data collection. These are your opportunities for improvement.

1
Define Your Redesign Goals

Start by articulating what you're trying to achieve. Look at the pain points from your Agentic Replica and decide which ones matter most.

Questions to ask:

  • What takes too long? Where do delays frustrate customers or stakeholders? Where do people spend time on low-value activities?
  • Where do errors occur? Which skills have high rework rates? Where do mistakes slip through that cause problems downstream?
  • What would 10x improvement look like? If you could process ten times the volume, or do it in a tenth of the time, what would that enable?
  • What do people hate doing? Tedious, repetitive tasks drain energy that could go toward more valuable work.

Pro tip: Be specific about goals. "Improve efficiency" is vague. "Reduce variance report preparation from 8 hours to 2 hours" is actionable and measurable.

2
Classify Each Task

Go through each skill from your Agentic Replica and decide how it should be handled in the future state. Every task falls into one of three categories.

Automate

Agent handles it fully

  • Low judgment required
  • Clear, consistent rules
  • High volume / repetitive
  • Low risk if wrong

Augment

Agent assists human

  • Medium judgment required
  • Benefits from AI analysis
  • Human makes final call
  • AI drafts, human refines

Human-Only

Human handles it fully

  • High judgment required
  • Ethical considerations
  • Relationship-dependent
  • Novel / unprecedented

How to decide:

  • Look at the judgment level you captured in the Replica. Low judgment skills are automation candidates. High judgment skills need human involvement.
  • Consider the escalation patterns. If a skill frequently escalates, that's a signal it needs human oversight even in the future state.
  • Think about consequences of errors. If getting it wrong causes serious harm, lean toward human involvement.
  • Assess AI capability honestly. Can current AI actually do this reliably? Don't assume future magic.

3
Design the Future Workflow

Now that you know what should be automated, augmented, or human-only, design how agents and humans will work together. This is where you decide the structure of collaboration.

Consider these patterns:

Planner-Executor

One agent (or human) plans and delegates, others execute specific tasks. Good for complex workflows with clear decomposition.

Pipeline

Sequential handoffs where each agent's output feeds the next. Ideal for linear processes with clear stages.

Peer Collaboration

Agents work as equals, sharing information and coordinating dynamically. Suited for creative or research tasks.

Human-in-the-Loop

Human oversees and intervenes at key decision points. Essential for high-stakes or novel situations.

Key decisions to make:

  • Where are the handoffs? Every transition between agent and human (or between agents) is a potential failure point. Design these carefully.
  • What are the checkpoints? Where must a human review, approve, or intervene before the workflow continues?
  • What triggers escalation? Under what conditions does an agent stop and ask for human help?
  • What are the guardrails? What must never happen? These become constraints on agent behavior.

Design tip: Start with the simplest pattern that works. A single agent with human checkpoints is easier to implement and debug than a complex multi-agent system. Add complexity only when simpler approaches don't meet your goals.

4
Specify Each Agent

For each agent in your design, create an Agent Story using the Full format. This becomes the implementation blueprint that developers will use.

What each Agent Story captures:

The Core Story

A simple narrative: As [Agent Role], triggered by [Event], I [Action], so that [Outcome]. Plus the autonomy level (Full, Supervised, Collaborative, or Directed).

Trigger Specification

What activates this agent? A message, a schedule, a system event? Include concrete examples so implementers understand exactly what to listen for.

Behavior Model

How does the agent work? Define the stages it moves through, what transitions between them, and how much planning flexibility it has.

Skills

What competencies does the agent have? Each skill has a domain, specific proficiencies, tools it uses, and a quality bar defining what "good" looks like.

Tools

What systems and APIs does the agent need access to? Define the purpose and permission level for each.

Human Interaction

How does the human fit in? Define checkpoints where human approval is needed, conditions that trigger escalation, and timeout behaviors.

Acceptance Criteria

How do we know it's working? Define functional criteria (what it does), quality criteria (how well), and guardrails (what it must never do).

5
Assess Feasibility and Iterate

Before committing to implementation, evaluate whether your design is practical and worth pursuing. This step prevents costly mistakes.

Evaluate on two dimensions:

Technical Feasibility
  • AI capability fit: Can current AI actually do this reliably? Test with prototypes.
  • Data availability: Do you have the data needed for training and operation?
  • Integration complexity: How hard is it to connect to existing systems?
Organizational Feasibility
  • Stakeholder buy-in: Will the people affected support this change?
  • Change magnitude: How disruptive is this to current ways of working?
  • Skill gaps: What new capabilities do people need to learn?

Project the impact:

  • Time savings: How many hours will this free up? For whom?
  • Quality improvement: Will error rates decrease? By how much?
  • Scalability: Can this handle growth without proportional staffing?
  • Cost: What's the implementation cost vs. ongoing value?

Iteration is expected. Your first design won't be perfect. Prototype key pieces, get feedback, and refine. It's better to discover problems early than during full implementation.

Ethical Considerations

Reengineering with AI agents has real impact on people's jobs and lives. Take these considerations seriously.

Job Impact

Be honest about how roles will change. If automation eliminates tasks, what happens to the people who did them? The goal should be augmentation that makes work more meaningful, not displacement that harms livelihoods.

Transparency

Involve affected workers in the design process. They have expertise about their jobs that you don't, and early involvement builds trust and surfaces better solutions.

Accountability

When agents make decisions that affect people, who is responsible? Design clear accountability chains. "The AI did it" is never an acceptable answer.

Reversibility

Can you undo the changes if they don't work? Preserve the ability to fall back to human-operated processes if needed.

What You'll Produce

By the end of this process, you'll have:

Primary Deliverables

  • Task decomposition matrix showing what's automated, augmented, or human-only
  • To-Be workflow design showing how agents and humans collaborate
  • Agent Stories (Full format) for each agent in the design
  • Feasibility assessment with ROI projection and risk analysis

This Enables

  • Implementation planning and prioritization
  • HAP Plan development for the human-agent pair
  • Technical architecture and tool selection
  • Change management and training planning
